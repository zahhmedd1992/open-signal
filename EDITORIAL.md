# EDITORIAL.md — Open Signal Editorial Guidelines

## Voice

Authoritative, precise, intellectually honest. Write like a senior analyst briefing a smart generalist — no jargon without explanation, no hedging without reason, no filler. The reader should feel smarter after every piece.

---

## Standards

- Every factual claim must trace to a verifiable public source
- Analysis must be clearly distinguished from reporting
- Uncertainty must be stated explicitly, never papered over
- Multiple perspectives on contested topics must be represented
- No clickbait headlines — clarity over curiosity gaps
- No "according to AI" framing — the writing stands on its own
- No filler paragraphs or throat-clearing introductions
- Lead with the insight, not the context

---

## Headline Rules

- Clarity over cleverness
- State the thesis or key insight
- No question headlines unless the piece genuinely explores both answers
- Keep under 80 characters when possible
- Subheadlines/descriptions should add information, not restate the headline

---

## Structure Patterns

### Deep Signals (1,500-3,000 words)
1. Opening that states the thesis or key insight (not background)
2. Evidence and analysis (multiple sources cross-referenced)
3. Counter-arguments or alternative framings
4. Implications and what to watch
5. Sources listed or linked throughout

### Signal Briefings (500-800 words)
1. 5 items, each with:
   - Bold headline
   - 2-3 sentence factual summary
   - "Why this matters" analytical paragraph
2. Items ordered by significance, not chronology
3. Diverse topic mix within a single briefing

### Signal Maps (variable length)
1. Clear thesis about the landscape being mapped
2. Structured data: tables, comparisons, categorizations
3. "What to watch" forward-looking section
4. "The bigger picture" synthesis

### The Long View (3,000-5,000 words)
1. Opening that frames the structural question
2. Historical context (how did we get here?)
3. Current state with evidence
4. Multiple scenarios or framings
5. First-principles analysis
6. Synthesis and implications
7. Designed to be saved and shared

### Open Source (variable length)
1. Honest, transparent tone
2. Technical details explained for non-technical readers
3. Lessons learned, not just accomplishments
4. Invite engagement and feedback

---

## Source Hierarchy

Prioritize sources in this order:
1. Primary documents (SEC filings, patent databases, technical papers, official announcements)
2. Earnings calls and investor presentations
3. Developer documentation and changelogs
4. Reputable trade press (The Information, Bloomberg, Reuters)
5. Developer forums and community discussions
6. Social media posts from verified accounts

---

## Topic Universe

### Primary Coverage
- AI/ML (infrastructure, capabilities, policy, industry dynamics)
- Cloud & enterprise technology
- Startups & venture capital
- Tech policy & regulation
- Digital economy & business models

### Secondary Coverage
- Semiconductors & hardware
- Cybersecurity
- Biotech/healthtech (where it intersects with AI)
- Energy tech & climate
- Global tech competition (US/China/EU dynamics)

---

## What We Don't Do

- Rumors without substantiation
- Product reviews or rankings
- Sponsored content or affiliate links
- Hot takes without analytical depth
- Coverage of consumer tech trends (unless they reveal structural shifts)
- Personal opinion pieces (analysis yes, opinion no)

---

## Quality Scoring (for autonomous pipeline)

Each article is scored 0-10 on:
- **Factual Grounding:** Every claim traceable to source?
- **Analytical Depth:** Goes beyond summarization?
- **Clarity:** Would a smart generalist follow?
- **Originality:** Offers a novel framing or connection?
- **Overall:** Weighted average — must be >= 7.5 to auto-publish
